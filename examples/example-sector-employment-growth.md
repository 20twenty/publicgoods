### Surprises and notable findings

**1. Healthcare is the real story — not the obvious one**

Leisure & Hospitality headlines at +27.8%, but that's misleading: March 2021 was a COVID trough, and the sector has only just returned to its pre-pandemic level (~17M). The genuine structural winner is Education & Health Services — it added the most jobs in absolute terms (+4.26M) and grew 18% from an already-healthy baseline. Drilling deeper:

- Ambulatory care (doctor's offices, outpatient clinics): +17.7% (+1.38M)
- Hospitals: +15.0% (+526k)

An aging population + deferred pandemic care + expanded insurance coverage is compounding into a multi-year hiring boom that's mostly flying under the radar.

---

**2. Construction at +12.6% — driven by policy, not housing**

This is above the economy-wide average despite mortgage rates crushing residential construction. The CHIPS Act, Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, and Inflation Reduction Act funneled hundreds of billions into domestic semiconductor fabs, bridges, and clean energy — all labor-intensive. Specialty trade contractors and heavy civil construction are where the growth is concentrated.

---

**3. Federal Government is shrinking (-7.9%) even as government overall grows**

Government overall grew +6.7%, but the federal civilian workforce contracted by ~227,000 jobs (-7.9%) from 2021–2026 — with DOGE-era reductions accelerating that trend through 2025–2026. State and local governments (education, police, transit) are what pushed the sector-wide number positive.

---

**4. Information sector: essentially flat (+0.8%) despite the AI era**

The tech narrative vs. the payroll data don't match. The 2022–2023 wave of Big Tech layoffs (Meta, Google, Amazon, Microsoft) erased the pandemic hiring binge, and AI/automation appears to be limiting headcount recovery even as revenues and profits have soared. The Information sector added only 21,000 jobs over five years — fewer than most single months for healthcare. Productivity-per-worker is up sharply; headcount is not.

---

**5. Manufacturing barely grew (+3.0%)**

Despite "reshoring" rhetoric and significant policy support, manufacturing added only 362,000 jobs. Automation and capital investment are absorbing the production expansion without proportional headcount growth — consistent with the broader productivity story in goods-producing industries.
